Product Concept · Google Maps

Google Maps:
Explore by Intent

A concept for shifting Maps from a navigation tool to an exploration companion, by understanding why someone is searching, not just what they typed.

most frequented restaurants by locals
01 — Executive Summary

The summary

Challenge

Google Maps excels at getting people from A to B. It wasn't built for the moment before that, when someone doesn't know where to go yet. Its search still runs on keywords, its ranking ignores who's asking, and it has almost no AI-driven personalization in the discovery layer.

Solution

An intent-based discovery experience: natural language search, moment-based collections, recommendations that explain themselves, and a lightweight profile system that matches you with people who have similar backgrounds and taste so their discoveries surface in your results.

Impact

Well-executed personalization typically lifts engagement 10–15% (McKinsey, 2021). That's what this concept is centered around, improving user experience and filling the main gap that exists within Maps.

02 — Context & Problem

Where Maps falls short

Business situation. Navigation is a solved problem. Now, the new competition is discovery, and Maps is losing it to apps that were never built for maps at all.

Google Maps today
  • Keyword search only
  • Sorts by relevance, distance, prominence
  • No user profile or preference signals
  • Reviews sorted by recency or helpfulness
  • No explanation for why a place is surfaced
Where people go instead
  • TikTok, Instagram: visual, peer-driven
  • Reddit: community, honest, contextual
  • Blogs & Substack: curated local guides
  • Yelp: filters but still keyword-dependent
  • Instagram: business following showcases target demographic
The gap
  • No true navigation features
  • No live hours or real-time data
  • Saving is too wide spread across apps
  • No aggregated reviews or ratings
  • No easy filtering when needed

"I don't want the highest-rated restaurant. I want the one locals would actually recommend."

— research interview, solo traveler, 24

User problem. Today Maps ranks everything by relevance, distance, and prominence, which mostly means review volume and keyword matches. It treats every search as a transaction, without any real intent behind the results. Someone searching "brunch" might want a two-hour catch-up with a friend or a quick plate before a flight. The result is the same either way.

  • Tourist-heavy spots crowd out what locals actually recommend
  • Generic results disconnected from the moment someone is in
  • Hard to read a neighborhood's personality before showing up
  • Decision fatigue: too many similar options, no reason to pick one
03 — Approach & Process

Getting to the idea

Hypothesis. If Maps understands who is searching and why, not just what they typed, it can compete for the moments people now spend on other apps, and it has something those apps don't: the ability to actually get you there.

Research method. Reviewed discovery experiences across Google Maps, TikTok, Instagram, Reddit, Airbnb Experiences, Yelp, and Apple Maps. Interview questions focused on real decisions: when was the last time you spent 10+ minutes deciding where to eat, how do you actually find restaurants while traveling, what makes you skip Maps entirely.

Decision 01

Conversation over keywords

Replace the search bar with natural language input. Instead of triggering filters, a typed or spoken intent shapes the entire result set.

"pizza" → "late-night pizza with outdoor seating, somewhere not too loud"
Decision 02

Moments over categories

Instead of Restaurants / Coffee / Bars, surface collections built around what someone is actually trying to do.

Restaurants, Coffee, Bars → Golden Hour Views · Date Night · Rainy Day · Slow Sunday Morning
Decision 03

Explain, don't just rate

A star rating tells you nothing about whether a place fits your moment. Recommendations should explain why this place, for this search.

★ 4.7 · 842 reviews → "People stay ~2 hrs · mentioned for the patio and live music"
04 — Profile System

Discovery through people like you

The answer to improved discovery isn't just keyword matching, it's knowing who's asking. Right now Maps treats every user identically. This concept adds a simple, opt-in profile layer that does two things: it makes your own results more relevant, and it contributes to a network where your saves and visits help others with similar profiles discover places too.

How profile signals flow. When you save a new location, that action gets attached to your profile attributes: UA student, 21, tends to save dive bars and late-night spots. Someone else who just moved to Tucson, also a UA student, gets this location surfaced in their results because people with similar profiles liked it, not because it ranked high on review volume.

Privacy consideration. All profile attributes are opt-in during onboarding and easy to edit or disable. The matching layer uses aggregated signals, not individual tracking. "12 students from UA saved this" is a segment signal, not a named list.

All metrics above are directional targets, not measured results. This is a concept project. The profile matching system in particular should be piloted carefully — the value of the network effect only emerges once enough users opt in, so early rollout should focus on contexts where that density already exists, like college campuses or dense urban neighborhoods.

Business impact. Social apps have won the browsing moment. But they can't route you, show you live hours, or let you save a place and walk in the door in one tap. Maps can do all of that, it just hasn't competed on the discovery layer yet. A profile system that matches you to people with similar backgrounds turns Maps from a utility into a trusted source, which is the position TikTok and Reddit currently own.

What I'd do differently. Test conversational search and moment collections separately before combining them. Launch the profile onboarding only on a segment with high save-behavior (frequent travelers, recent movers) where the taste graph seeds quickly.
